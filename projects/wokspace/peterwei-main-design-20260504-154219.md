## Problem Statement

People who want to self-host services on their own hardware are blocked by residential ISPs that provide rotating/dynamic IPs. There is no middle ground between "self-host at home with a dynamic IP" and "rent a VPS from AWS." The founder wants to build a community data center — a physical space with rack rental, managed local services, and a cafe that serves as a trust layer and distribution channel.

## Demand Evidence

Founder is the first customer. The pain is personal: "I want to host my own, but I won't use all the resources personally, but I can't give people access because residential areas have rotating IPs; so I don't host my own; this is the gap I'm trying to address."

No external demand validation yet. The r/homelab and r/selfhosted communities confirm the dynamic IP problem is widespread, but desire is not willingness to pay. This landing page exists to bridge that gap.

## Status Quo

People who want to self-host currently:
- Use workarounds: Cloudflare Tunnels, Tailscale, dynamic DNS, WireGuard meshes
- Give up and rent VPS from AWS/GCP/Hetzner
- Don't self-host at all

Current workarounds are free and "good enough" for most people. The people for whom they're not good enough are the target market.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

**Target user:** Technical individuals and small businesses who want data sovereignty and self-hosting but are blocked by residential internet limitations. They want to run their own hardware on a static IP with physical access.

**Narrowest wedge:** A landing page with email capture that describes the full vision and measures whether people will commit their email (and optionally pre-pay for a founding membership). The waitlist IS the product at this stage.

## Constraints

- Must work as a standalone landing page, not dependent on the existing Clojure app
- Must capture email addresses reliably
- Must lead with ONE value proposition (rack rental with static IP) to get a clean signal. Other props (managed services, cafe) shown as "also coming"
- Must be deployable in under a day (founder time: ~4 hours. CC generation: ~30 min)
- Mobile-first responsive design

## Premises

1. Enough people in the founder's area want to self-host on their own hardware but can't due to residential internet limitations to sustain a physical space. **Founder has conviction based on personal experience and defended this against an independent challenge.**
2. The cafe/physical third-place is load-bearing, not decorative. People choose this over traditional co-lo because they can walk in, sit down, and know the person running their servers.
3. Both managed services AND rack rental should be offered from day one (in the full buildout, not on this landing page).

## Cross-Model Perspective

An independent Claude subagent reviewed this session cold. Key findings:

- **Steelman:** "A neighborhood data center that makes self-hosting a social activity instead of a lonely infrastructure project. The cafe is the distribution channel. The real product is trust and proximity."
- **Challenged Premise 1:** Desire is not willingness to pay. Most people who hit the dynamic IP wall find that Cloudflare Tunnel or Tailscale is good enough. The gap between "I wish I could self-host" and "I will pay real money and change my behavior" is enormous.
- **Recommended prototype:** Skip everything physical. Build a landing page with a waitlist and Stripe "founding member" link. Answer the only question that matters: will anyone pay for this?
- **Founder defended Premise 1** against this challenge, choosing to keep it with specific reasoning.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Validate-First Landing Page (CHOSEN)
- Summary: Waitlist + landing page describing the full vision. Captures paying interest before building anything physical.
- Effort: S (human: 1 day / CC: 30 min)
- Risk: Low
- Pros: Answers demand question in a weekend. Costs nothing.
- Cons: Measures interest, not demand. People sign up for ideas they forget.

### Approach B: Server-in-My-Garage MVP
- Summary: Rack in founder's own space with business internet. Real customers, real money. Micro-scale validation.
- Effort: M (human: 1-2 weeks / CC: 2 hours)
- Risk: Medium
- Pros: Validates with real money. Founder is the first customer.
- Cons: Residential zoning may not allow commercial internet. Limited scale.

### Approach C: Full Vision, Phased Build
- Summary: Landing page (2 weeks) → rack rental from commercial space (1-2 months) → full cafe buildout (6+ months).
- Effort: L (human: 3-6 months / CC: 1-2 days cumulative)
- Risk: Medium-High
- Pros: Honors all three premises. Phased go/no-go gates.
- Cons: Requires discipline to not skip phases.

## Recommended Approach

**Approach A: Validate-First Landing Page.** Deploy a single-page site at networkcafe.ai with:
1. Clear value prop in the hero: "Self-host on your own hardware with a static IP you can walk to."
2. Email capture via Resend (free tier, up to 100 emails/day). Each signup triggers a confirmation email with "what's next" copy.
3. One primary value proposition (rack rental with static IP). Managed services and cafe shown as "also planned" below the fold.
4. Two tiers shown as prospective pricing (NOT purchasable yet): Waitlist $0, Rack+Services $30/mo. These gauge willingness-to-pay, not real products.
5. FAQ addressing key objections (physical access, network specs, legal, location, why not Tailscale)
6. Stripe Payment Link (no-code, one-time $15 founding member pledge, NOT recurring subscription). Position as pledge/donation, not pre-sale. Include clear "refund anytime" language.

**Wireframe layout (single page, mobile-first):**
- Nav: logo + anchor links (Pricing, FAQ)
- Hero: headline + subhead + email capture (email + city fields) + founding member pledge link below + social proof counter (hidden until 10+ signups, then shows exact number from serverless endpoint)
- Value props: 3 cards (rack rental primary, managed services secondary, cafe tertiary)
- Tiers: 2 prospective pricing cards (Waitlist, Rack+Services) — labeled "Planned pricing — not yet available"
- FAQ: 5 questions (copy to be provided by founder)
- Footer: tagline + one-line privacy statement inline (not a separate page)

**Tech stack:** Plain HTML/CSS/JS (no framework). One Netlify Function (`/api/signup`) that: validates email, stores to a Google Sheet via Sheets API (free, simple, founder can view directly), calls Resend API to send confirmation email, returns success/error. No database.

**Analytics:** Plausible (free 30-day trial, privacy-friendly). Track: page views, email submissions, referral source.

**Privacy:** One line in the footer: "We store your email and city to notify you about launch in your area. We won't share it. Unsubscribe anytime."

**Post-capture flow:** Netlify Function calls Resend API to send templated confirmation: "You're on the list for [city]. We'll reach out when we have enough interest near you. Reply and tell us what you'd host."

**Stripe founding member pledge:** Stripe Payment Link (one-time $15, manually created in Stripe dashboard — no code). Linked from a button below the email form: "Support the build — become a founding member ($15)." Separate from the prospective pricing tiers. Refund policy: "Full refund anytime before we open. Email us."

## Open Questions

- Where will the physical space be? City/neighborhood determines the customer base.
- What's the business internet cost in that area? This determines pricing floor.
- How many rack units can you fill before needing to expand? Unit economics matter.
- What managed services launch first? Email and LLM access are in the codebase, but Nextcloud/file hosting is not.

## Success Criteria

- 30+ email signups within 2 weeks of launch (adjusted: realistic for local-first distribution)
- 5+ founding member pledges ($15 one-time)
- At least 3 replies to the confirmation email describing what they'd host
- Geographic concentration: at least 10 signups listing the same metro area (captured via city field on signup form, stored in Google Sheet)

**Failure mode:** If fewer than 15 signups in 2 weeks, the signal is "not enough local demand." Do not proceed to physical space. Instead, broaden to a remote/managed services play or reconsider the market.

## Distribution Plan

- Deploy to networkcafe.ai domain (already owned)
- Static site on Netlify (free tier)
- **Primary channels (local):** local hackerspace Slack/Discord, city subreddit (r/[city]), local tech Meetup groups, coworking space bulletin boards
- **Secondary channels (awareness):** r/homelab, r/selfhosted (with city name in title — filters for locals), HN "Show HN" post
- The waitlist counter creates urgency once past 10 signups

## Dependencies

- Resend account (free tier, up to 100 emails/day) for confirmation emails
- Stripe account for founding member pledge Payment Link (created in dashboard, no code)
- Google Sheets for email/city persistence (free, founder-viewable, no database needed)
- Netlify account (free tier) for hosting + one serverless function
- Plausible account (30-day free trial) for analytics
- Domain DNS pointing to Netlify

## The Assignment

Before building the page: write a text post describing what you're considering ("I'm thinking about opening a rack-rental space with static IPs in [city]"). Post it in your local hackerspace's Slack/Discord and city subreddit. If nobody replies to a text post, the landing page won't convert either. If you get replies, build the page and send them the link.

## Session Reflections (non-normative — not implementation guidance)

It took two more questions to get to the actual problem: "residential areas have rotating IPs; so I don't host my own." The pain is sharper than the vision. Lead with the pain next time you pitch this.
- You chose the validate-first path over the "just start building" path. That's unusual for a technical founder. Most engineers would pick Approach B (set up the rack, figure out demand later). You picked the path that answers the hardest question first. Good instinct.
- You defended Premise 1 without hedging. The second opinion made a legitimate statistical argument ("most people find workarounds good enough") and you kept your conviction. That matters — but only if you're right. The landing page will tell you.
