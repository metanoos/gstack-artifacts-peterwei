# CEO Plan: Trace-Native Writing Pivot

## Executive Verdict

Pivot Zine around one foundational thesis now:

> Final text is a lossy compression of the writing process. An AI that receives
> the current text plus relevant, structured trace evidence can collaborate
> with the writer's actual trajectory better than an AI that receives the
> current text alone.

This is Approach C plus continuous evidence gathering. Evidence does not grant
permission to orient the product around the thesis; that strategic decision is
made. Evidence measures the size and boundaries of the advantage, identifies
which trace signals help or hurt, and determines how the system should evolve.
Negative results must be published and must change selectors, prompts, memory
scope, interaction design, or sequencing when the data warrants it.

The product hierarchy becomes:

1. **Daily-use loop:** trace-aware collaboration that helps a person write,
   revise, continue, and understand a work without repeatedly reconstructing
   their intent for the AI.
2. **Initial buyer wedge:** accountable teams working on durable AI-assisted
   artifacts whose reviewers need to understand what happened.
3. **Defensible substrate:** signed, portable, replayable process evidence that
   belongs to the author rather than one model provider.

The existing multi-AI task and correspondence plan remains useful execution
infrastructure. This pivot changes its product center of gravity: task turns,
exact context, adapters, replay, and handoff are not only an accountability
system; they are how present and future AIs receive better evidence about the
work in progress.

## Vision

### 10x Check

The ordinary product records AI-assisted writing so someone can audit it later.
The 10x product makes the record useful during creation. A writer invokes any
supported AI and it can see not merely the latest arrangement of words, but the
relevant path that produced them: a direction repeatedly removed, a phrase
deliberately preserved, a source introduced late, a model passage heavily
rewritten, a structural experiment reverted, or a convention consistently
chosen within this file or folder.

The AI does not receive an undifferentiated history dump. A shared,
task-specific compiler selects bounded evidence, renders an exact manifest,
and shows the writer why every item was included. The writer can remove an
observation, correct it, limit it to one operation, remember it for a file or
folder, or explicitly promote it to a user-wide preference. The resulting AI
operation is durably bound to the approved context manifest, so replay can show
what the AI actually received as well as what it returned.

The same trace can serve different models without locking the writer into one
provider. Improvement in foundation models increases the value of the preserved
signal because more capable models can make better use of the same author-owned
evidence.

### Platonic Ideal

Zine feels like a writing instrument that has paid attention without pretending
to know the writer's mind. The person remains in the text, not in a generic chat
pane. AI operations feel less repetitive and less generic because they respect
observable decisions already made in the work.

Before dispatch, the writer can answer:

1. What current text will the AI see?
2. Which trace moments were selected, and why are they relevant to this task?
3. Which remembered preferences apply here, at what scope, and from what
   evidence?
4. Which provider, model, instructions, tools, and context budget are involved?
5. What will remain private, what will be stored, and what could later be sent?

Afterward, the writer can answer:

1. Did trace-aware assistance reduce the work needed to reach an acceptable
   result?
2. Which selected evidence affected the response?
3. Did the AI overfit to an old choice, repeat a rejected direction, or infer
   beyond observable evidence?
4. Can the exact operation be reconstructed without trusting the provider?
5. Should any observation be corrected, forgotten, narrowed, or promoted?

## Premise Boundaries

Zine treats trace as evidence of observable process, not a psychological
profile. Deletion can show that text left the work; it does not prove why.
Timing can show elapsed time; it does not prove hesitation or attention.
Repeated choices may justify a scoped preference proposal; they do not justify
silently declaring a permanent trait.

The thesis is strongest for task-local process facts and weakest for broad,
cross-project inference. The architecture must preserve that confidence
gradient. Raw signed trace remains authoritative evidence. Compiled
observations, relevance scores, summaries, and preferences remain derived,
versioned, inspectable, correctable, and non-normative.

## What Already Exists

- `apps/client/src/ai/context-block.ts` already injects current file/folder text,
  directory history, span payloads, mechanical edit magnitudes, and process
  summaries into every direct single-shot model operation.
- `apps/client/src/ai/prepared-operation.ts` already freezes exact messages,
  fingerprints dependencies, computes `preparedRequestHash`, and prevents a
  stale approval object from silently changing before dispatch.
- `apps/client/src/ai/context-snapshot.ts` already provides deterministic input
  ordering, completeness checks, byte budgets, and a context fingerprint.
- `apps/client/src/ai/PromptInspectorModal.tsx` already exposes the exact frozen
  messages approved for a direct operation.
- `Analyze` already receives an exact validated trace-process log and is
  instructed to bind interpretations to observable evidence.
- KEdit capture, replay, signed Steps, model voices, context relationships, MCP
  writing, and the shared protocol kernel provide the raw substrate.
- The narration study already shows one bounded result: structured trace
  evidence improved fidelity when a model described how an artifact was made.

The missing system is not trace capture. It is task-specific selection,
portable compilation, scoped memory, durable operation binding, correction,
and causal product evaluation.

## Accepted Scope

### Execution posture: full destination, gated lakes

This is one product pivot, not one implementation branch. The accepted scope
is split into independently reversible epics with explicit gates:

1. internal manifest contract and cross-runtime golden vectors;
2. deterministic task-local selection for two desktop operations;
3. inspection, operation-only exclusion, and explicit file-local memory;
4. minimal local binding plus counterfactual dogfood;
5. reviewed durable protocol binding;
6. folder and user memory with lifecycle semantics;
7. MCP and provider-adapter portability; and
8. longitudinal external evaluation.

No epic waits for evidence to declare the overall thesis strategically
important. Each later epic does wait for the preceding contract, safety, and
usability gates so the project does not build five interpretations of an
unstable primitive. Phase 2 remains private disposable dogfood until the
durable-binding trust review passes; it is not a generally released state.

### Shared trace-context runtime

Create a versioned package outside the normative protocol kernel. It consumes
validated trace views plus an operation/task descriptor and emits a frozen
context manifest containing:

- the target text identity and current content hash;
- selected source Step, transaction, and span references;
- mechanical observations separated from calibrated inferences;
- applicable scoped preferences and their evidence;
- inclusion/exclusion reasons, scores, budgets, and shielding decisions;
- compiler, selector, renderer, and prompt-layer versions; and
- a deterministic fingerprint over the exact approved manifest.

The runtime must support a text-only baseline, a bounded chronological-trace
condition for research, and the shipping task-selected condition without
maintaining divergent model-operation codepaths.

Private payload access is abstracted behind `TraceContextPrivateStoreV1` with
closed capabilities: `putEncrypted`, `resolveAuthorized`, `pin`, `unpin`,
`prune`, `keyedDedupLookup`, and `prepareDisclosure`. The manifest compiler
depends on this interface, never on desktop Stronghold or an MCP filesystem
directly. One conformance suite covers authorization failure, missing blobs,
pin/reference counting, quota exhaustion, key loss, randomized ciphertext,
dedup isolation, erasure, and selective disclosure.

Desktop implements the capability behind its native secure-store boundary.
Each named MCP profile owns a separate encrypted local vault and distinct key;
keys are never copied from the desktop or another MCP profile. Browser builds
provide a read-only/null implementation and cannot prepare private-payload
operations. MCP retention remains disabled until its vault, OS-keychain or
passphrase recovery policy, filesystem permissions, backup behavior, and key
rotation pass a separate security review.

#### Internal manifest contract

`TraceContextManifestV1` is a closed, non-sensitive envelope before it is a
protocol shape. Exact text and preference values live in separately encrypted,
deletable payload blobs addressed by hash. The envelope's required fields are:

```text
version: 1
policy: text-only-v1 | bounded-trace-v1 | selected-trace-v1
target: { traceId, headId, contentHash, chosenPath, ancestorTraceIds[] }
operation: OperationDescriptorV1
selected: ContextEvidenceV1[]
preferences: AppliedPreferenceV1[]
exclusionSummary: { countsByReason, firstBudgetRejectedRef?, auditHash?, auditBlobRef? }
payloadRefs: EncryptedPayloadRefV1[]
traceWindow?: { headId, includedOldestNodeId, includedNewestNodeId,
                includedStepCount, omittedPrefixCount,
                omittedPrefixCommitment }
budget: { effectiveContextBytes, usedContextBytes,
          candidateCount, selectedCount, truncated }
completeness: { complete, failures[] }
versions: { compiler, selector, renderer, promptLayers[] }
hashes: { frozenInputs, renderedContext }
```

The local stored record is
`{ encryptedEnvelopeRef, manifestCommitment, commitmentSaltRef,
envelopeByteLength }`. The exact
envelope is encrypted at rest. `manifestCommitment` is SHA-256 of the domain-
separated preimage
`zine.trace-context.manifest.v1\0 || random256BitSalt || JCS(envelope)` and is
therefore neither self-referential nor practically guessable while the salt
remains private. The signed operation Step binds this commitment, not the
plaintext envelope or salt. A deliberate evidence packet may disclose the
envelope and salt so an independent reviewer can verify the commitment.

`envelopeByteLength` is measured only after canonical envelope serialization
and therefore lives outside the commitment preimage. Readers recompute it for
integrity, quota, and diagnostics; a mismatch is storage corruption, not a new
manifest identity.

Private payload commitments use the same fresh-salt pattern. A separate local
deduplication index uses `HMAC-SHA-256(profileDedupKey, exactPlaintextBytes)`;
that keyed index never enters a Step, manifest, export, or relay event. This
allows randomized payload encryption and local deduplication to coexist.
Algorithms and domain labels are pinned in golden vectors rather than inherited
from ambient helper defaults.

Arrays have defined stable order; sets are sorted by raw bytewise UTF-8 order
with no locale comparison or case folding. Structured values use RFC 8785
canonical JSON. Document bodies are hashed as their exact UTF-8 bytes and are
never Unicode-normalized by the compiler. Paths retain their exact stored POSIX
spelling; golden vectors include NFC and NFD names so runtimes cannot silently
normalize them. Byte budgets are canonical. Provider token estimates are
advisory metadata excluded from the manifest hash.

`ContextEvidenceV1` contains `{id, kind, source, claimClass, claimHash,
claimPayloadRef?, reasons[], priorityClass, sensitivity}`.
`source` contains exact trace/head/node IDs, optional transaction ID, and an
optional source-revision half-open UTF-16 span. `kind` is one of
`instruction|protected-range|correction|preference|process-fact|citation`;
`claimClass` is `explicit|mechanical|derived`. Selector V1 emits only explicit
and mechanical claims and carries no confidence field. Sensitivity is
`public|trace-private|profile-private`; any private claim value lives only in
the referenced encrypted payload.

`AppliedPreferenceV1` contains `{id, scope, valueHash, valuePayloadRef,
evidenceRefs[], explicit, precedence, status, sensitivity}`. Scope is
`{kind:file|folder|user, traceId?}`; `traceId` is required for file/folder and
forbidden for user. `explicit` is always true in V1. Status is
`active|overridden|conflicted`; every non-active candidate remains present.

`EncryptedPayloadRefV1` contains `{hash, kind, byteLength, retentionClass}` and
never a plaintext value. Retention class is
`attempt-transient|bound-operation|preference|research`. The Inspector resolves
payloads only after local authorization and renders a missing blob as a
reconstruction gap.

Excluded candidates are not expanded in the envelope. A compact summary gives
counts by typed reason and the first budget rejection. An optional encrypted,
paginated audit attachment contains compact source references for the full
candidate set; its hash is bound by the envelope. The envelope has a 512 KiB
hard ceiling and one audit attachment has a 4 MiB ceiling.

`traceWindow` is required only for `bounded-trace-v1` and forbidden for the
other policies. Starting at the prepared head, the policy admits the newest
complete conforming Steps that fit, then renders that suffix in forward chain
order. `omittedPrefixCommitment` is the domain-separated SHA-256 commitment to
the canonical ordered omitted node-ID list; the encrypted envelope protects
the trace-window metadata until disclosure. Empty omission uses count 0 and the
commitment to an empty list. Golden vectors cover no omission, one omitted Step,
large omission, invalid Step exclusion, and exact-boundary fit.

Timestamps used as evidence remain signed source values. Manifest generation
time is metadata excluded from deterministic identity. Given identical frozen
inputs, versions, and policy, desktop and MCP must produce byte-identical
canonical envelope bytes and hashes.

Determinism does not mean an unfrozen LLM may make relevance decisions during
compilation. V1 candidate generation and selection are mechanical. A future
model-derived observation must first become a frozen candidate carrying its
exact input hash, provider/model, prompt version, returned bytes/hash, and
confidence; the compiler then treats that candidate deterministically.

A future derived selector may add a versioned confidence field only after a
preregistered calibration study defines its target event, scoring rule,
calibration corpus, reliability metrics, and calibration-version identifier.
Uncalibrated ordinal or numeric confidence is forbidden.

`OperationDescriptorV1` is also closed:

```text
op: extend | settle
targetTraceId: exact trace identity
targetHeadId: exact prepared head
range: optional UTF-16 start/end in target body
explicitIntent: optional exact user instruction
protectedRanges: ordered UTF-16 start/end spans in target body
maxContextBytes: integer hard ceiling
reservedPromptBytes: exact non-context request bytes
```

Extend normally has no range and selects evidence about the target's recent
trajectory and explicit scoped constraints. Settle has a required range and
selects conservative file-level process evidence plus protected ranges and
applicable explicit preferences. All ranges are half-open UTF-16 code-unit
offsets into the exact prepared target head, may not split a surrogate pair,
and fail preparation when invalid. Historical evidence retains offsets in its
own named source revision and is never remapped into the current body in V1.
Adding Stir, Reply, Analyze, or agent contracts requires a new descriptor
fixture rather than overloading these two.

#### Selector V1

V1 is deliberately conservative and avoids revision-aware span lineage.
Candidate evidence is limited to validated conforming Steps and their already
materialized process summaries on the target's linear `prev` chain, explicit
corrections/preferences, protected current ranges, and directly cited context
already approved for the operation. It does not infer psychology, latent
durable traits, historical/current span equivalence, reverts, or repeated
rewrite regions.

Selection uses fixed priority classes in this order: explicit operation
instruction; protected range; applicable correction; explicit scoped
preference; process facts on the prepared target head; process facts on prior
target Steps; directly cited source Step. Target Steps are ordered by distance
from the prepared head along its unique validated `prev` chain (distance 0
first), then transaction index, then raw UTF-8 node-ID bytes. Cited sources
retain approved `q` order, then raw node-ID bytes. Duplicate evidence references
collapse to one item while retaining every reason for inclusion. Revert or
cross-revision region detection is reserved for a later selector version with
persistent span-lineage infrastructure and separate evidence.

The selector fills the byte budget in priority order. Mandatory current text,
explicit instructions, and protected ranges are never truncated. If mandatory
content alone exceeds the hard ceiling, preparation fails visibly instead of
silently dropping it. The effective rendered-context ceiling is
`min(operation.maxContextBytes, 256 KiB,
PREPARED_REQUEST_MAX_BYTES - reservedPromptBytes)`. Exact UTF-8 bytes of current
text, instructions, protected text, and rendered selected evidence count toward
that ceiling. Envelope metadata and exclusion audit attachments do not, but
they are subject to their independent 512 KiB and 4 MiB ceilings. Selected
items and the first budget-rejected reference remain in the envelope; the rest
are represented by typed counts and the optional paginated audit attachment.

Selector V1 has a closed process-fact mapping:

| Fact kind | Permitted fields | Claim class | Sensitivity | Canonical rendering |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `step-summary` | transaction/range counts, inserted/deleted code-point counts, first/last captured transaction time, span, longest gap, undo/redo counts | mechanical | trace-private | `Step <node> · <N> tx / <R> ranges · +I/−D · span S · longest gap G · undo U · redo R` |
| `transaction` | source node/revision, transaction ID/index, captured time, explicit KEdit intent when present, change count, sorted exact voice IDs | mechanical | trace-private | `tx <id> @ <time> · <intent?> · <N> changes · voices <ids>` |
| `change` | source node/revision, transaction ID/index, operation, half-open source UTF-16 range, inserted/deleted code-point counts, exact voice ID, inserted/deleted encrypted payload refs | mechanical | trace-private | source line plus resolved `− <deleted>` and `+ <inserted>` payload lines |

Only `TraceProcessView.status === complete` from a conforming Step may produce
these facts. Invalid and snapshot-only records appear in completeness/exclusion
metadata but never as persuasive process claims. A selected `change` always
includes its containing transaction and Step reference. Exact inserted/deleted
text counts toward rendered-context bytes and remains in encrypted payloads;
the envelope carries only salted commitments and references. Empty values omit
their line. Golden vectors cover insertion, deletion, replacement, multi-range,
multi-voice, undo/redo, invalid, empty-history, Unicode, and budget truncation.

No V1 fact names retention, rejection, intent beyond an explicitly recorded
KEdit intent, influence, preference, or motive. Those require a separate
derived observation version and evidence gate.

### Inspectability and correction

The Prompt Inspector evolves from showing messages to explaining trace context.
For every selected item it shows the observable claim, source Step/span, scope,
confidence, reason for inclusion, and estimated context cost. The user can:

- exclude it from this operation;
- mark it irrelevant or incorrect;
- remember an explicit preference for this file;
- remember it for a folder subtree, including the root folder;
- explicitly promote it to a user-wide preference; or
- forget/revoke a durable preference.

Corrections are themselves versioned local evidence. They never rewrite the
raw trace and never disappear merely because the compiler changes.

Correction types have distinct effects:

- **exclude once** is ephemeral and removes one evidence item from the pending
  operation only;
- **irrelevant here** creates a scoped selector suppression for this operation
  type and file, retaining the disputed source reference;
- **incorrect observation** invalidates one derived observation version but
  never the raw Step/span it cited;
- **preference override** creates a more-specific explicit preference whose
  precedence is resolved by the scope lattice; and
- **forget** revokes a durable preference or suppression for future manifests
  while retaining the minimum tombstone needed to explain historical
  manifests that used it.

Every correction records target identity, correction type, scope, creation
version, and optional superseded correction. Conflicting live corrections are
shown and block automatic promotion. Selector-learning experiments may consume
exported corrections only with separate consent; ordinary correction does not
silently train a remote system.

Durable corrections use a closed local `CorrectionV1` record:

```text
version: 1
id: random 128-bit lowercase hex
type: irrelevant-here | incorrect-observation | preference-override | forget
target: { manifestCommitment?, evidenceId?, observationVersion?, preferenceId? }
scope: { kind: file | folder | user, traceId? }
operation: extend | settle | any
status: active | superseded | revoked | orphaned
createdSeq: monotonic per-profile integer
createdAt: local display metadata
applicability: { fromHeadId?, throughHeadId? }
supersedes: correctionId?
payloadRef: encrypted correction value/reason reference?
```

Exactly one target identity is required for each correction type. File/folder
scope requires its trace ID; user scope forbids one. `createdSeq`, not wall
clock, establishes local order. Supersession is explicit and acyclic; a newer
record does not override another merely by existing. A target outside its
validity window is retained for historical inspection but ineligible for new
selection. Missing target evidence marks the correction orphaned. Tombstones
retain only ID, target hash, former scope/type, and revocation/supersession
links after private payload erasure.

Precedence is: valid explicit correction over a derived observation; more
specific structural scope over broader scope; operation-specific over `any` at
the same structural scope; explicit supersession over the superseded record.
Two incompatible active corrections still tied after that tuple block
preparation under D8.

### Scope lattice

```text
operation (ephemeral; never remembered automatically)
    |
    v
file trace
    |
    v
folder trace and its current descendant subtree
    |
    v
user-wide preference (explicit approval only)
```

The root is an ordinary folder whose subtree happens to cover the workspace.
Effective preferences compose from broad to narrow: user, root/ancestor
folders, nearest folder, then file. The most specific applicable preference
wins deterministically for dispatch; every overridden or conflicting candidate
remains visible in the manifest rather than being silently collapsed.

Two incompatible active preferences at the same effective scope are a hard
preparation conflict, not a selector tie. The Inspector shows both records and
requires the writer to supersede, narrow, revoke, or exclude one for the current
operation before dispatch. No creation-time, lexical-ID, or model-mediated
tie-breaker may turn equal-scope conflict into implied intent.

File preferences are keyed by file trace identity and follow that trace when it
moves. Folder preferences are keyed by folder trace identity and apply through
current containment. Moving a file therefore preserves its file memory while
changing inherited folder memory; the next context manifest discloses that
change. User-wide memory is local and explicit until separately reviewed sync
semantics exist.

No preference is promoted upward automatically. The system may propose a
promotion only with repeated supporting evidence and an explicit user action.

Identity and containment lifecycle rules are explicit:

- rename or move that preserves a trace identity preserves file memory;
- copy/duplicate creates a new trace identity and receives no file memory unless
  the user explicitly imports selected preferences;
- delete tombstones file memory locally; restoring the same trace identity can
  reactivate it after review, while a new trace at the old path cannot;
- folder rename/move preserves folder preferences when folder identity is
  preserved;
- folder reparenting changes inherited ancestor preferences at the next
  manifest and discloses the before/after scope set;
- a file mounted through more than one live containment path resolves against
  the path chosen for the operation and records that path in the manifest;
- orphaned evidence remains inspectable but ineligible for new selection until
  its signed source is reachable and valid again; and
- attaching a different root changes folder inheritance but never user-wide or
  file-identity memory.

Folder and user scopes do not ship until these cases, conflict precedence,
revocation, and orphan display pass dedicated tests. Phase 2 exposes only
operation exclusion and explicit file-local memory.

### Durable operation evidence

Every accepted AI result must bind to the exact approved context manifest,
prepared request hash, provider/model configuration, prompt-layer versions,
target revision, and result hash. This should join the planned Phase 1 schema
cut and two-commit model-operation lifecycle rather than create a parallel
history system.

The durable record distinguishes:

- hashes and references sufficient to identify exact evidence;
- local private payloads required for faithful reconstruction;
- packet-eligible material the user has approved for transfer; and
- public/sent material.

An ordinary Send never acquires private trace context implicitly. Sharing an AI
result does not automatically share every input or remembered preference that
influenced it.

“Reconstruction” has bounded tiers:

1. **input-complete:** exact approved envelope plus every referenced private
   payload, rendered messages, request
   bytes/hash, target revision, and known configuration are available;
2. **execution-observed:** Zine retained the provider frames and lifecycle
   events it actually observed;
3. **tool-state referenced:** external tools/resources have immutable captured
   content or exact references; and
4. **output-complete:** exact returned and accepted bytes are available.

No tier promises deterministic regeneration from a changing hosted model,
hidden provider state, mutable network resource, or uncaptured tool. The UI
states the achieved tier and its gaps.

#### Private-data lifecycle

The immutable manifest envelope never embeds target text, evidence bodies,
preference values, or rendered messages; it binds their hashes and encrypted
payload references. Envelope references and hashes may live as long as the
bound Step. Exact private prompt/context payloads are local-only by default and stored in an
encrypted per-profile evidence store whose data-encryption key is protected by
the native secure-store boundary. The trust/schema review must validate that
storage design before non-disposable use; browser builds remain read-only and
cannot create durable private payloads.

Failed, rejected, cancelled, and unbound shadow-operation payloads use
`attempt-transient` retention and are garbage-collected after 30 days. Payloads
bound to an accepted Step remain pinned while that trace exists; content-
addressed deduplication prevents identical trace bodies from being copied per
operation. A profile has a default 2 GiB private-evidence quota: warn at 80%
and block new payload-producing dispatch at 100% rather than silently prune
bound evidence. The user may raise the quota or explicitly prune selected
payloads after reviewing which historical operations will lose
`input-complete` status.

Research outcome payloads default to 30-day retention unless the participant
chooses a shorter period or explicitly contributes a redacted case. Preferences
persist until revoked; revoked values are erased while a non-sensitive
tombstone preserves historical explainability. “Forget” cannot alter an
already signed event or retract material the user deliberately Sent, and the UI
must say so before confirmation.

Exports are deny-by-default for prompt bodies, private linked text,
preferences, corrections, and evaluation assignments. Backup and sync are
separate consent surfaces. Deleting a private payload leaves the historical
operation marked `payload unavailable`; it must never be represented as
input-complete afterward.

Garbage collection is hash-reference-aware and never deletes a blob still
pinned by a live bound operation or active preference. Deleting a trace offers
to delete now-unreferenced bound blobs. Secure-store key loss makes encrypted
blobs permanently unavailable while leaving envelope hashes verifiable; the UI
downgrades reconstruction rather than offering a false recovery promise.

#### Versioning and historical inspection

Canonical non-sensitive envelope bytes are immutable and retained with their
version; old manifests are inspected from stored envelope bytes rather than regenerated with a new
compiler. New compiler/selector versions create new manifests. There is no
in-place semantic migration.

Readers preserve an unsupported historical manifest as raw canonical envelope bytes,
verify its recorded hash, and show its version as unsupported without using it
for new selection. The minimum retained historical set is envelope bytes,
rendered-context hash, prepared request hash, target/head identifiers, selected
evidence references, version set, and reconstruction-tier status. Any future
protocol promotion needs cross-version golden fixtures before release.

### Counterfactual evidence system

Evaluation is a first-class, consented runtime capability. It supports
counterbalanced assignment among:

1. **text-only:** exact current target text, explicit user instruction,
   protected spans, and explicitly cited source bodies; no delta log, trace
   summary, prior prompt, origin label, derived observation, or preference;
2. **bounded chronological trace:** condition 1 plus the newest suffix of the
   validated chronological target process log that fits the same hard byte
   ceiling; omitted prefix count and hash are recorded; and
3. **selected trace:** condition 1 plus Selector V1 evidence and applicable
   explicit scoped preferences within the same ceiling.

Every condition is generated by one manifest runtime and has a golden fixture
proving which fields are present. A baseline fails validation if any
trace-derived field leaks into condition 1.

The product should not routinely show three outputs or triple provider cost.
Shadow comparisons are an explicit research/dogfood mode. Ordinary use records
only user-approved outcome signals and never sends a second request silently.

Primary outcome measures:

- blind or order-counterbalanced author preference;
- amount and type of editing required before acceptance;
- time to acceptable result;
- preservation of declared intent, protected phrases, and structure;
- recurrence of previously rejected directions;
- acceptance, partial use, rejection, and later reversion;
- perceived control and ability to explain why context was selected;
- latency, token usage, and provider cost; and
- over-personalization, irrelevant memory, repetition, and sycophancy.

Every result is stratified by operation, model/provider, trace length, writer,
and compiler version. Research reports distinguish direct user outcomes from
model-graded proxies and preserve negative or null findings.

Operational definitions for the first preregistration:

- an **acceptable result** is an output the author explicitly marks ready to
  use after zero or more edits;
- **partial use** means the author explicitly inserts a selected subset and
  that subset remains present at the next deliberate Step;
- **edit burden** is UTF-16 insertions plus deletions between generated output
  and the accepted stepped form, normalized by generated length;
- **later reversion** means at least 80% of an accepted AI span is removed
  within the next three Steps or 24 hours, whichever comes first;
- **repeated rejected direction** requires a user-marked rejection linked to a
  later suggestion judged to reproduce the same direction under a blinded
  rubric by two independent human raters, with a third blinded adjudicator on
  disagreement; model grading is reported only as a proxy and never resolves
  the primary label; and
- missing outcomes, provider failures, abandonment, and user withdrawal are
  reported separately and never silently counted as preference wins.

The live study randomizes one condition per real operation opportunity, blocked
within writer by operation and model family; the same live target opportunity
is never exposed to two conditions. A separate frozen-task study may generate
paired outputs and present them in blinded, counterbalanced order. Provider,
prompt, and compiler drift are blocked or modeled by version and never pooled
as though identical.

Phase 0 performs a power calculation for the preregistered minimally important
effect, with a floor of 30 evaluable live operations in each operation-by-model-
family cell: Extend and Settle across at least two capable model families, for
at least 120 evaluable operations from at least 12 writers. The power
calculation may raise but never lower that floor. A broad
product claim requires the 95% confidence interval to exclude no improvement
and either at least a 10-point trace-aware preference advantage or a 15%
relative reduction in median normalized edit burden in both operations, with a
positive estimate and no preregistered material-harm boundary crossed in either
model family. A cross-model claim additionally requires a positive family-
specific interval in both families. These
thresholds may be revised only before enrollment and remain published with the
result. Cost, latency, privacy burden, and correction burden are co-primary
guardrails, with exact thresholds preregistered from baseline measurements.

Carryover controls include blinded paired presentation, counterbalanced order,
no repeated live target across conditions, writer-level blocking, and explicit
recording of prior exposure to the experimental UI. Withdrawal removes future
collection and any deletable private payload without altering already
published aggregate results.

Memory receives a separate staged preregistration after selected trace clears
the core benefit gate. It holds compiler, selector, operation, model family,
and context budget constant, then randomizes explicit file-local memory disabled
versus enabled across longitudinal operation opportunities. The same writer-
blocking, per-cell power calculation, missing-data rules, and guardrails apply.
Phase 5 cannot infer memory value from self-selected usage or enter on the core
trace study alone.

## Performance and Resource Ceilings

V1 candidate retrieval is indexed by trace identity, reachable head, linear
Step-chain distance, correction/preference scope, and direct citation. It must
not replay an unbounded history on every keystroke. Compilation is cancellable,
incremental by head ID, and cached by frozen-input fingerprint.

Initial hard ceilings are 10,000 candidate transactions examined, 256 KiB of
rendered context, a 512 KiB envelope, a 4 MiB paginated candidate-audit
attachment, and the existing prepared-request ceiling. Exceeding the
candidate ceiling produces a visible incomplete/failure state rather than a
false complete manifest. Before Phase 2 exits private dogfood, benchmark empty,
100-Step, 1,000-Step, and 10,000-transaction traces on the development machine;
set published p95 preparation budgets from those measurements and add them to
regression tests. Provider token estimates and latency are measured separately
from deterministic byte compilation.

CI also runs a fixed corpus for deterministic correctness and relative
performance regression, without treating shared-runner wall time as the user
latency claim. Before Phase 3 general release, the release matrix names a
representative low-end supported device and preregisters p50/p95 preparation
budgets for the same four corpus sizes. Cancellation, quota failure, incomplete
candidate ceilings, cache-cold, and cache-warm behavior are exercised at every
size. Failing the low-end p95 gate blocks general release or narrows the
supported platform claim.

## Architecture Review

### Component and dependency graph

```text
signed Trace events
        |
        v
packages/protocol: validate + produce ValidatedTraceView
        |
        v
trace-context package: index + Selector V1 + renderer + envelope
        |                    |                         |
        |                    |                         +--> TraceContextPrivateStoreV1
        |                    +--> CorrectionV1 / scoped preference inputs
        v

        |                                               |
        v                                               v

                                                            binds salted commitment

desktop adapter: native private vault       MCP adapter: per-profile private vault
browser adapter: read-only/null store        provider adapters: no storage ownership
```

The normative protocol package must not import the trace-context package. The
context package consumes a transport-neutral `ValidatedTraceView` and cannot
publish, sign, call a provider, read a filesystem, or access a vault directly.
Applications inject operation descriptors, validated views, private-store
capabilities, and explicit preferences/corrections. Provider adapters consume
only an approved prepared request and never rebuild context.

This coupling is intentional: every press shares deterministic context
semantics while raw evidence remains usable without the compiler. A compiler
failure cannot invalidate or mutate the trace.

### Manifest lifecycle

```text
GATHERING
   | complete validated inputs
   v
PREPARED --dependency changes--> INVALIDATED
   | inspect + approve                |
   v                                  +--> re-gather only
APPROVED --dependency changes--> INVALIDATED
   | dispatch
   v
IN_FLIGHT --provider terminal failure--> FAILED (attempt retained; no Step)
   | complete bytes
   v
RESULT_READY --target CAS fails--> STALE_RESULT (retained; no auto-apply)
   | human accepts
   v
BOUND (MODEL Step commits result + salted manifest commitment)
   |
   +--private blob pruned/key lost--> BOUND_DEGRADED
                                      (Step valid; reconstruction tier lowered)
```

Impossible transitions are rejected: gathering cannot dispatch; prepared
cannot apply; approval cannot survive a dependency fingerprint change; partial
or failed output cannot become a MODEL Step; stale output cannot auto-apply;
payload deletion cannot erase or reinterpret a signed Step.

### Scaling, failure isolation, and rollback

The first pressure point is candidate retrieval on long traces, followed by
encrypted payload storage and Inspector rendering—not signature verification or
provider transport. Indexed, incremental compilation and hard candidate,
context, envelope, attachment, and private-store quotas bound each stage.
Desktop and MCP vaults fail independently; one cannot decrypt another.

The shared compiler is a high-leverage logical dependency but not an artifact
single point of failure: writing, stepping, replay, and text-only operations
remain available. A selector regression rolls back by pinning the prior
compiler/selector version for new manifests. Existing envelopes are immutable
and inspected with their recorded versions. A protocol-binding regression uses
the feature flag to stop new trace-aware dispatch while preserving signed
Steps, attempts, and local payloads for forward repair. No rollback rewrites or
silently downgrades historical evidence.

**Architecture review result:** boundaries are coherent after decisions
D7–D16. No unresolved Section 1 finding remains; security, typed rescue, test,
and rollout details continue in their dedicated sections.

## Error and Rescue Registry

`TraceContextErrorV1` is a closed discriminated union. Every variant carries:

```text
version: 1
code: closed enum
stage: gather | validate | select | render | store | approve | dispatch | apply | bind
affected: { traceId?, headId?, nodeId?, pathHash?, preferenceId?, correctionId? }
retryable: boolean
degradation: none | newly-prepared-text-only
userDetail: redacted structured fields
diagnosticRef: encrypted local detail reference?
```

Free-form private text never enters `userDetail`, logs, metrics, or error labels.
Consumers switch exhaustively on `code`; unknown future errors fail closed and
render as unsupported rather than falling through to a generic retry.

| Method/codepath | Typed failure codes | Rescue action | User sees |
|---|---|---|---|
| `gatherValidatedView` | `TARGET_MISSING`, `TARGET_NOT_STEPPED`, `ANCESTRY_UNAVAILABLE`, `TRACE_INVALID` | Missing/unstepped blocks; unavailable/invalid trace may offer a separately gathered text-only manifest | Focus/Step instruction or exact reader verdict; never a false full-trace claim |
| `compileCandidates` | `CONTEXT_INCOMPLETE`, `CANDIDATE_LIMIT_EXCEEDED`, `UNSUPPORTED_INPUT_VERSION` | Incomplete/limit fails selected policy; offer new text-only preparation; unsupported input requires compatible reader | Missing identities/counts and allowed next action |
| `selectV1` | `PREFERENCE_CONFLICT`, `CORRECTION_CONFLICT`, `INVALID_SCOPE`, `INVALID_RANGE` | Resolve/exclude conflict or repair operation descriptor; no automatic tie-break | Both conflicting records or invalid half-open range |
| `renderContext` | `MANDATORY_BUDGET_EXCEEDED`, `CONTEXT_BUDGET_EXCEEDED`, `ENVELOPE_LIMIT_EXCEEDED`, `AUDIT_LIMIT_EXCEEDED` | Optional candidates truncate deterministically before error; mandatory/envelope/audit hard failure requires narrower scope or omitted audit attachment | Exact ceiling and largest safe contributors; no payload text in error |
| `privateStore.putEncrypted` | `PRIVATE_STORE_UNAVAILABLE`, `PRIVATE_STORE_QUOTA`, `PRIVATE_STORE_KEY_LOST`, `PRIVATE_STORE_PERMISSION` | Unavailable/permission may retry after repair; quota requires prune/raise; key loss permanently downgrades old blobs and blocks new writes until rekey | Vault state, affected reconstruction tiers, and safe remediation |
| `prepareOperation` | `DEPENDENCY_CHANGED`, `UNSUPPORTED_MANIFEST_VERSION`, `COMMITMENT_FAILURE` | Invalidate and rebuild from current dependencies; version requires supported compiler; commitment failure blocks | Which dependency class changed, never hidden payload |
| `approveOperation` | `APPROVAL_STALE`, `APPROVAL_MISMATCH` | Discard approval and reopen exact new Inspector state | Old/new manifest commitment prefixes and changed categories |
| `attemptJournal.begin` | `JOURNAL_UNAVAILABLE`, `ATTEMPT_CONFLICT` | Do not dispatch; retry idempotently after journal repair | “AI request not sent” plus attempt ID |

No catch-all handler may swallow one of these failures. Boundary adapters may
wrap an unexpected runtime exception as `INTERNAL_UNCLASSIFIED`, attach only an
encrypted diagnostic, block dispatch/apply, and fail the contract test until a
specific code is added.

The real-relay and crash-boundary suite must interrupt each path before and
after journal, provider, payload-store, CAS, signing, event-store, and link
reconciliation boundaries. The invariant is: no provider dispatch without a
durable AUTHOR intent/attempt; no accepted text mutation without its atomic
MODEL Step; no retry creates a second application of the same result.

**Error/rescue review result:** D17 closes the shared error taxonomy and rescue
contract. No silent failure or unclassified happy-path degradation is accepted.

## Security and Instruction Authority

Trace-derived text is untrusted data by default. `InstructionAuthorityV1`
classifies every rendered segment as either `instruction` or `quoted-data` and
records its origin, exact source range, acting identity, operation scope, and
classification reason. Only the explicit current operation instruction,
approved ordered prompt-rule layers, and deliberately authorized `((…))`
directives may be `instruction`. Current prose, protected quotations, KEdit
insertions/deletions, citations, prior AI output, external paste, preference
evidence, and historical commands are `quoted-data`.

Provider renderers preserve segment boundaries and wrap quoted data in
provider-appropriate structured/delimited containers without changing its
bytes. The system layer states that quoted segments are evidence rather than
commands, but security does not rely on that statement alone: origin/scope
classification, Inspector disclosure, minimal tool authority, output review,
and atomic acceptance remain independent controls. Sanitizing or deleting
instruction-like evidence is forbidden.

Adversarial fixtures cover instruction phrases in deleted text, protected
quotes, citations, EXTERNAL paste, prior MODEL output, another author's trace,
preference values, file paths, and provider/tool responses. No such phrase may
enter the instruction segment merely because its bytes resemble a command.

### Delimiter grammar

- `[[ … ]]` is protected quoted material. Its exact bytes survive eligible
  transformations, it remains `quoted-data`, and directive-looking text inside
  it is inert. Existing minted/citation forms retain their more-specific
  provenance semantics; protection never promotes them to instructions.
- `(( … ))` is a consumed AI directive candidate. When provenance and operation
  scope authorize it, the inner text enters the explicit instruction layer;
  the delimiter and command are not emitted into transformed prose.
- Delimiters are parsed from the exact prepared target revision before context
  compilation. Their source ranges and authority decisions appear in the
  manifest and Prompt Inspector.
- Historical, cited, pasted, or MODEL-origin `(( … ))` remains literal quoted
  data unless the acting writer deliberately re-authors/promotes it through an
  authorized current-target action.
- Cross-nesting is invalid in V1 except that any delimiter bytes inside a
  complete `[[ … ]]` span are literal. Unbalanced active `((`/`))` syntax blocks
  preparation with a visible range; it never half-promotes text to instruction.
- The operation descriptor identifies which target range is eligible. A
  directive outside that range is ordinary data for that operation.

Directive authority follows D18.1: only a complete `((…))` span in the exact
prepared target range whose entire Run is manually authored by the acting local
AUTHOR may activate. MODEL, EXTERNAL, FILESYSTEM, another AUTHOR, cited,
historical, or mixed-origin spans remain quoted data. Explicit promotion creates
a new acting-AUTHOR edit/record; it never mutates the source origin label.

Authority derivation follows D20. Desktop maintains a local, non-normative
`AuthoritySpanV1` sidecar over the exact current text revision. Edit capture
classifies inserted ranges as `manual`, `paste`, `drop`, `import`, `filesystem`,
`model`, or `explicit-promotion`; adapters may add a closed future origin only
through a versioned contract. Only `manual` and `explicit-promotion` spans are
instruction-eligible. The complete opening delimiter, inner payload, and
closing delimiter must be covered by eligible spans from the acting AUTHOR;
mixed or missing coverage is inert.

The sidecar is local evidence, never a signed KEdit field or protocol claim.
The compiler accepts generic authority spans and remains ignorant of editor
event APIs. The desktop adapter derives them from direct editor transaction
annotations and explicit Inspector actions. Copy, paste, drop, import,
filesystem reconciliation, MODEL application, and content whose sidecar cannot
be verified all fail closed. Inspector promotion authorizes the exact current
span for the current actor and revision without relabeling the bytes' historical
origin; any byte or boundary change invalidates that promotion.

Sidecar persistence follows D20.1. Desktop stores the current authority map
locally per profile, keyed by stable trace identity plus exact head/content
hash. A successful Step rekeys the verified map to the new head; reload restores
it only when trace, head/content hash, and flattened text agree. File moves keep
the map because trace identity is stable. Local editor transactions map spans
forward with the same UTF-16 coordinates as the text edit, including metadata-
aware undo/redo; ambiguity invalidates the affected span instead of guessing.

Copy, paste, drop, import, merge adoption, and cross-file duplication create
inert origin spans at the destination even when the bytes match an authorized
source. Filesystem reconciliation invalidates every affected range. A different
device/profile receives no authority from the signed trace and therefore shows
the candidate as inert with an explicit promotion action. Sidecars are not
synced or exported in V1, contain no plaintext directive payload, and prune
superseded revision maps after their in-flight reservations and crash-recovery
records close.

Directive extraction uses a regex only as an optional candidate prefilter. The
authoritative implementation is a single linear scanner over the prepared
target revision plus provenance Runs: recognize complete protected `[[…]]`
spans first, treat everything inside them as literal, find non-nested `((…))`
candidates outside them, validate balanced boundaries and Run authority, then
intersect with the operation range. This avoids regex-only failures on protected
or malformed content while remaining O(n).

Each authorized directive becomes a frozen `DirectiveV1`:

```text
id: deterministic ordinal in source order
source: { traceId, headId, pathPayloadRef, fromUtf16, toUtf16, authorPubkey }
instructionPayloadRef: encrypted exact inner-text bytes
marker: deterministic non-authoritative placeholder
operation: extend | settle | stir | reply | analyze | run
authority: acting-author-current-target-v1
localAnchor: { containingRange, beforeHash, afterHash, excerptPayloadRef }
```

The quoted target body sent to the model replaces each consumed directive with
its marker so the directive's position remains legible without giving the body
instruction authority. A dedicated ordered `AUTHOR DIRECTIVES` instruction
section contains each directive's exact text, source marker, and bounded local
anchor. The marker and raw `((…))` syntax are output-ineligible; if either
returns, result review flags it before acceptance rather than silently deleting
model output.

Directive locality follows D18.2. The primary anchor is the marker at the exact
source position in the complete quoted target body. The directive record also
includes the smallest containing blank-line-delimited paragraph/block, clipped
to the prepared operation range, as an explicitly labelled `quoted-data`
excerpt. The excerpt is context for interpreting positional references such as
“this sentence,” “above,” or “the paragraph here”; it never inherits the
directive's instruction authority.

The excerpt has a preregistered UTF-8 byte ceiling. If the containing block
exceeds that ceiling, the compiler emits deterministic balanced before/after
windows around the marker without crossing the operation range, preserving
complete Unicode scalar values. At an empty or block boundary it selects the
nearest non-empty in-range block with the deterministic preference
`containing -> preceding -> following`. Directives in the same block retain
distinct markers and source ranges but share one content-addressed excerpt in
the rendered request and manifest. The full target body remains available once;
local excerpts are not repeated as unlabelled prompt prose.

Operation coverage follows D19. The delimiter grammar is universal across
prepared AI operations: Extend, Settle, Stir, Reply, Analyze, and Run may each
activate an authorized in-range directive. This is one product contract with
staged implementation, not a requirement to release every adapter together.
Each operation must define its target/source range, prompt placement, result
shape, consumption lifecycle, Inspector representation, and adversarial fixture
before its adapter may ship. An operation that cannot interpret a directive
under its closed descriptor fails preparation visibly rather than silently
reclassifying it.

For Reply, Analyze, and Run, instruction authority does not grant new product or
tool capabilities. The directive may guide the operation only within the
already approved operation scope, tool policy, filesystem boundary, network
policy, and output contract. Capability expansion still requires the ordinary
explicit approval path; document bytes cannot confer it.

Directive lifecycle follows D19.1: authorized `((…))` directives are one-shot
for every operation. Preparation stages exact source-range deletions but does
not mutate the target. PREPARED, APPROVED, IN_FLIGHT, and RESULT_READY attempts
reserve their directive IDs so another operation cannot activate them
concurrently. Cancellation, provider failure, rejected output, invalidation, or
staleness releases the reservation and leaves the source bytes unchanged.

Accepted Settle/Stir results combine directive deletion and replacement in one
editor transaction. Accepted Extend results combine directive deletion and
continuation insertion in one transaction. Reply and Analyze require a
journaled idempotent commit group that binds source deletion and result-artifact
creation; Run binds source deletion to its accepted terminal receipt. A crash
recovery path resumes the same request/Directive IDs and may not dispatch the
model or repeat tool effects.

Cleanup failure follows D22. Once accepted success writes the durable
consumption receipt, that receipt—not the presence of delimiter bytes—is the
semantic authority. If source deletion fails, the successful result remains,
the directive renders consumed/inert, and cleanup retries idempotently without
provider dispatch or repeated tool effects. The degraded state exposes the
linked receipt and cleanup error. Rollback may not reactivate the directive or
claim that external Run effects were reversed.

Undo semantics follow D21. An accepted consumption receipt is durable across
editor undo/redo and remains authoritative even if undo restores the original
`((…))` bytes. Restored bytes render as a consumed, inert candidate linked to
the accepted operation; they cannot reserve or dispatch again. `Reactivate` is
an explicit Inspector action that creates a new local directive identity,
authority record, and future attempt lineage for the exact current bytes. It
never reopens or reuses the accepted request ID.

Same-file undo naturally reverses the atomic text/result transaction but does
not erase the historical receipt; redo reapplies the local transaction without
a provider call. Cross-artifact undo does not silently delete a Reply, Analyze,
or Run result, and can never claim to reverse external Run effects. Result
artifacts remain linked and may be deleted through their ordinary explicit
workflow.

Protection precedence follows D18.3. `[[…]]` is an absolute invariant for the
prepared operation and cannot be overridden by a `((…))` directive, prompt
rule, preference, correction, or model output. A mechanically detectable
directive/protection conflict fails preparation with a typed error naming both
source ranges. When a semantic conflict cannot be proven before dispatch, result
review compares every protected span byte-for-byte against the prepared target
and blocks acceptance on any change. Changing protected material requires a new
acting-AUTHOR edit that first removes or narrows the protection; there is no
same-operation escape hatch or textual-order precedence.

**Security review finding D18:** resolved by the instruction-capability
firewall, explicit delimiter grammar, D18.1 acting-AUTHOR authority rule, and
D18.2 position-preserving bounded local anchors. **Security review finding
D18.3:** resolved by absolute protected-span precedence and pre-dispatch plus
pre-acceptance enforcement. **Product decision D19:** resolved in favor of a
universal directive grammar with separately gated operation adapters and a hard
capability ceiling. **Product decision D19.1:** resolved in favor of one-shot
consumption only after accepted, journaled success. **Security decision D20:**
resolved with a local-only authority-span sidecar, explicit promotion, and
fail-closed treatment for pasted, imported, generated, mixed, or unknown bytes.
**Security decision D20.1:** resolved with same-profile persistence across
Steps, reloads, and moves; no cross-device authority; deterministic edit
mapping and fail-closed invalidation. **Interaction decision D21:** resolved
with durable consumption receipts across undo/redo and explicit new-identity
reactivation. **Interaction decision D22:** resolved by separating durable
semantic consumption from retryable textual cleanup; successful work is never
repeated because source deletion failed.

- Define the package boundary and input/output contracts.
- Adapt existing validated trace, context snapshot, shielding, and budget code.
- Reproduce current text-plus-structured-log behavior as a compatibility
  baseline before adding selection.
- Add fixtures proving deterministic output across desktop and MCP readers.

### Phase 2 — One complete desktop vertical slice

- Integrate Extend and Settle first because continuation and revision expose
  different trace-value mechanisms.
- Show selected evidence in the existing Prompt Inspector.
- Support operation-only exclusion and explicit file-local memory. Folder and
  user scopes appear as documented future choices but do not persist until
  their lifecycle and conflict gates pass.
- Preserve a text-only condition behind an explicit dogfood/research flag.
- Store minimal local envelope bytes, payloads, and hashes for disposable dogfood; do not
  represent this as final protocol binding or release it generally.

### Phase 3 — Durable binding and outcome capture

- Land the protocol/schema review needed to bind accepted model Steps to exact
  context manifests and prepared requests.
- Capture consented outcome events locally with export/redaction controls.
- Run the preregistered comparison across multiple models and real writing
  tasks.
- General release of trace-aware model operations is blocked on this phase's
  durable-binding and private-data lifecycle gates.

### Phase 4 — Generalize across operations and presses

- Enter only when Phase 3 shows a preregistered benefit for at least one initial
  operation, no material-harm boundary is crossed for the other, and the shared
  manifest has no unresolved privacy or correction critical gap.
- Move only operation classes supported by the measured mechanism onto the
  runtime; each of Stir, Reply, Analyze, cumulative Continue, and agent runs
  requires its own descriptor fixture and dogfood gate before general release.
- Thread equivalent bounded metadata through the MCP press.
- Require each provider adapter to pass one context-manifest contract suite.

### Phase 5 — Longitudinal scoped learning

- Enter only when explicit file-local memory improves a preregistered
  longitudinal outcome without crossing over-personalization, correction-
  burden, privacy, or latency guardrails.
- Add folder and user scopes, repeated-evidence proposals, explicit scope
  promotion, conflict display, expiry/review, revocation, copy/move/restore,
  reparenting, and orphan semantics.
- Evaluate whether remembered context improves later sessions without causing
  over-personalization.

### Phase 6 — External evidence and distribution

- Recruit consented writers and teams using real durable work.
- Publish outcome summaries and limitations in the evidence ledger.
- Use the accountability wedge for initial team distribution while measuring
  the daily writing-assistance loop directly.

## Success and Modification Criteria

The pivot is working when:

- trace-selected assistance beats text-only assistance on author preference
  and edits-to-acceptance across more than one operation and model family;
- writers can identify why context was used and successfully correct it;
- repeated use shows fewer recurrences of explicitly rejected directions;
- benefits survive longitudinal use without unacceptable over-personalization,
  latency, token cost, or privacy burden;
- the same manifest produces equivalent evidence claims across desktop, MCP,
  and multiple model adapters; and
- writers or teams return because the AI collaborates better, while reviewers
  independently value the durable provenance.

The thesis is modified—not protected from evidence—when:

- raw/full history hurts while selected evidence helps, requiring stricter
  task-specific retrieval;
- benefits appear only for some operations, trace ages, or model capabilities;
- durable memory hurts more than document-local trace, requiring narrower
  default scopes;
- users cannot understand or correct context selection cheaply enough; or
- provider cost and latency outweigh the measured writing benefit.

Reconsider the full product bet only after repeated, adequately powered tests
across real writers, tasks, and capable models show no useful advantage over
text-only context and no narrower trace mechanism produces one. One small null
study is a design signal, not sufficient grounds to abandon a foundational
information-theoretic premise.

## NOT in Scope

1. Psychological diagnosis, personality scoring, mood inference, or claims
   about unobservable intent.
2. Automatic user-wide preference inference or silent upward scope promotion.
3. Sending an entire raw trace to every operation regardless of task or budget.
4. Publishing private context, linked traces, corrections, or preferences when
   an output is Sent.
5. Treating derived context observations as signed protocol truth.
6. Human-authorship detection, cheating verdicts, or proof that no outside AI
   was used.
7. Capturing hidden provider prompts, chain of thought, or unobservable tool
   activity.
8. A provider-specific memory silo or architecture that cannot serve MCP and
   future compatible presses.
9. Silent shadow requests, hidden evaluation assignment, or unconsented
   research telemetry.
10. Global rendezvous, a public trace corpus, managed organization controls,
    or other network expansion before the trace-native writing loop works.

## Dream State Delta

Current Zine already captures unusually rich process evidence and injects much
of it into direct model operations. The missing delta is turning a client-local
chronological context dump into a shared, task-specific, user-governed context
runtime whose effects are durably bound and empirically measured.

The pivot therefore does not discard the existing product. It changes the role
of its strongest primitive. Trace stops being only something a later reviewer
examines and becomes active material through which a human and AI continue the
work together.

issues were then resolved explicitly through user decisions D7–D16 and remain
listed as an audit trail:

1. **RESOLVED by D15:** file memory receives a separate randomized longitudinal
   selected-trace-without-memory versus selected-trace-with-file-memory study;
   Phase 5 cannot enter on observational usage or the core trace study alone.
2. **RESOLVED by D9:** persisted corrections use the closed local
   `CorrectionV1` schema above, with explicit targets, scope, operation,
   applicability, supersession, tombstones, and encrypted payload references.
3. **RESOLVED by D7:** the envelope is encrypted locally and only a fresh-salted
   commitment enters durable Step metadata; disclosure of envelope and salt is
   a separate author-approved act.
4. **RESOLVED by D14:** `envelopeByteLength` lives in the outer stored record,
   outside the commitment preimage, and is recomputed by readers.
5. **RESOLVED by D13:** `bounded-trace-v1` requires the closed `traceWindow`
   above, renders the newest fitting suffix chronologically, and commits to the
   canonical omitted prefix.
6. **RESOLVED by D8:** incompatible active values at the same effective scope
   block preparation until the writer resolves or excludes one; arbitrary
   deterministic tie-breakers are forbidden.
7. **RESOLVED by D11:** the closed process-fact table above defines permitted
   Step, transaction, and exact change fields, sensitivity, canonical rendering,
   exclusion rules, and required golden vectors.
8. **RESOLVED by D10:** Selector V1 omits confidence entirely; a future derived
   selector may add it only with a preregistered calibration target, corpus,
   scoring rule, reliability report, and calibration-version identifier.

    Phase 3 general release is gated on preregistered p50/p95 budgets for a
