## Problem Statement
Synchrono City should help a founding organizer turn Christianity-adjacent curiosity into repeated in-person gathering. The first wedge is not a general Christian social app and not founder matching. It is a host-led set of recurring study circles, each with office hours that let a curious outsider reduce uncertainty before deciding to attend a real-world gathering.

The specific user truth surfaced in this session is simple: a person can be curious about Christianity, serious ideas, and philosophy, see a church or hear about a study, and still not go because the room is socially opaque. They do not know who is inside, what the norms are, whether they will feel trapped or judged, or whether the conversation will be rigorous enough to justify showing up. The founding organizer wants to run more than one recurring room over time, and the product should support that without collapsing those rooms into one generic audience blob.

## Demand Evidence
Current demand evidence is founder-led, not market-proven.

- The founder described a repeated hesitation loop: seeing a church or possible gathering, then deciding not to go because the people, norms, and social cost are unknown.
- The founder identified a sharper mechanism than "people need more religious content." The web already supplies infinite content. What is missing is a low-cost way to sample a specific room before committing an evening to it.
- Search across generalized category research supported the uncertainty-reduction mechanism. Broad religious-attendance research and church visitor material consistently point to pre-visit anxiety, uncertainty about social fit, and the importance of reducing ambiguity before an in-person visit.

Evidence gap:

- No named external organizer or attendee yet.
- No one outside the founder has said they would be upset if this disappeared.
- No evidence yet that this exact study-circle format converts curiosity into attendance.

## Status Quo
The current workaround is thin, indirect, and mostly solo:

1. A person becomes curious about Christianity through philosophy, geopolitics, online media, or general meaning-seeking.
2. They consume podcasts, videos, essays, or social posts alone.
3. They may see a church building, hear about a group, or consider attending a meetup.
4. They do not go, because they cannot cheaply sample the people, norms, intellectual level, or social pressure of that specific room.
5. Curiosity remains private and content-heavy, but does not convert into repeated embodied participation.

The real competitor is not "another Christian app." It is private curiosity plus passive online consumption.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge
Target user:

- The founding organizer is the first operator and first core user.
- The first attendee is a philosophy- and meaning-oriented adult who is curious about Christianity, wants a serious argument-driven room, and is unwilling to walk into an unknown gathering cold.
- They do not need more content. They need lower social risk around a specific room.

Narrowest wedge:

- One host in one city running a small set of recurring study circles, each anchored to a real place and cadence.
- Each circle has its own office hours online before the in-person gathering.
- A curious person can inspect host intent, thesis, norms, attendee expectations, prior questions, and group memory for that exact circle.
- They can join office hours quietly, listen first, ask a question if they want, and then decide whether to attend the corresponding in-person study.

This is narrower than "Christian community platform" and more concrete than "movement-building infrastructure." It is a trust ladder for a host-led cluster of recurring rooms.

## Constraints
- Must preserve Synchrono City's core thesis that online coordination should increase the odds of real-world gathering.
- Must keep the World map as the primary discovery and orientation surface rather than replacing it with a flat list or standalone landing-page product.
- Must treat place as first-class, not as a passive tag. The room should be anchored to a real recurring place.
- Must keep presence intentional. Users should be able to sample without broadcasting continuous location.
- Must support a host-led cadence. Ambient chat alone is too weak and likely to decay.
- Must let one organizer run multiple distinct circles without blurring their identities, norms, and archives together.
- Must feel low-risk for first-time attendees: join muted, easy exit, no forced intro, visible norms.
- Must reuse the current repository shape where possible: beacon/place model, call overlay, bootstrap-fed app state, and Concierge boundaries.
- Must make federation explicit as a product property and expansion path so other organizers can host their own circles later under their own infrastructure and rules.
- Must optimize for learning because the founder currently has worldview conviction but little external demand evidence.

## Premises
1. The first viable product is for a founding organizer running recurring study circles, not for church administrators in general.
2. The first attendee is someone who is curious and intellectually serious, but socially cautious about unknown rooms.
3. The main job is uncertainty reduction before in-person attendance, not content publishing and not broad networking.
4. A recurring office-hours format is a stronger trust surface than either a static landing page or a purely ambient group chat.
5. Ambient chat matters between sessions, but the hosted room is the primary product event.
6. If this product works, it should increase repeated in-person participation rather than trap people in an online-only community.
7. Federation matters, but as an architectural property and scale path, not as the emotional day-one wedge.
8. The world map remains the main lens: circles are discovered and understood spatially, not through a generic social feed or church directory.

## Approaches Considered
### Approach A: Group Landing Page MVP
Create one public page for a study group with host intro, thesis, norms, timing, place, and selected past summaries.

Pros:
- Fastest to ship
- Directly addresses some uncertainty
- Easy to test with one host

Cons:
- Weak live sampling
- Easy to collapse into "church website but nicer"
- Underuses the map, room, and continuity primitives already in the product

### Approach B: Beacon-Based Study Circle
Treat the study circle as a place-bound beacon with rich metadata, room memory, and persistent community context.

Pros:
- Strong fit with Synchrono City's place-first architecture
- Good long-term shape for repeated scenes
- Strong continuity and memory model

Cons:
- Still underspecified on the trust-ladder moment
- Risks becoming a social object without a clear low-cost sampling path
- Heavier than needed for the first learning loop

### Approach C: Study Circle Office Hours
The host runs recurring office hours attached to specific study-circle beacons. Curious attendees inspect the room, join quietly, ask questions, and decide whether to attend the in-person gathering. Ambient thread and memory support each room between sessions, while the same host can operate several distinct circles.

Pros:
- Best fit for the actual hesitation mechanism uncovered in the session
- Converts online sampling into offline attendance
- Reuses live presence, beacon, map, and continuity patterns already in Synchrono City

Cons:
- Requires real host reliability and cadence
- More host labor than a static page
- Needs careful moderation and clear norms to avoid drift

## Recommended Approach
Choose Approach C, Study Circle Office Hours.

This keeps the part of the prior founder-discovery design that still matters, the trust ladder, while replacing the wedge entirely. The product should not start as a broad Christian network or a movement platform. It should start as software for one host who can run a small constellation of recurring rooms, each with one honest question from the attendee: "Can I understand what this room is before I risk showing up?" The World map stays central. This is not a pivot away from map-native discovery. It is a refinement of what the map is for.

Recommended product shape:

1. The World tab remains map-first. Each recurring study circle appears as a place-bound beacon on the World map, even when multiple circles belong to the same host.
2. The map should let a curious user browse not just by doctrine or host, but by neighborhood, venue, and route, because place is part of the trust decision.
3. Opening a beacon reveals the host, thesis, norms, cadence, expected attendee mix, and recent group memory for that exact circle.
4. When office hours are live, the host is visibly present and curious users can join muted, listen first, and ask questions if they want.
5. Between sessions, an ambient thread preserves summaries, prior questions, and next-session context, but remains subordinate to the hosted room.
6. The product should make it easy for one organizer to run multiple circles without confusing audiences, archives, or expectations.
7. The in-person gathering remains the primary outcome. Online presence exists to reduce uncertainty and increase embodied return.
8. Federation should be visible in the design language and system model: other organizers should eventually be able to host their own circles and carry their identity, memory, and rules forward independently.

## Open Questions
- Should first-time attendees be allowed to join office hours pseudonymously, or should host identity symmetry require stable identity from everyone?
- Should office hours default to audio-first to lower social risk, or does visible video build more trust for this category?
- How explicit should the host's doctrinal thesis be on the entry surface before it starts filtering out too many curious people?
- Does the first in-person gathering need RSVP and attendance caps, or is soft intent enough?
- What moderation tools are required before opening the room to people outside the founder's immediate network?
- What is the cleanest mental model for one host running several circles: separate beacons with shared host identity, or a host-level hub with circle-level rooms beneath it?
- At what point should independent organizers on separate infrastructure be visible inside the same discovery surface?

## Success Criteria
- Recruit 5 to 10 curiosity-driven adults in one city to try the office-hours flow.
- Run 3 recurring office-hours sessions with the host reliably present.
- At least 60% of invited users who open the beacon inspect enough to understand the room before deciding.
- At least 40% of office-hours attendees later express intent to attend the in-person study.
- At least 2 in-person gatherings happen with repeat attendance from people first met through office hours.
- At least one second circle is legibly distinct from the first, proving that one host can run multiple recurring rooms without confusing users.
- Qualitative outcome: attendees report that being able to sample the host, norms, and room tone made the in-person visit feel safe enough to try.

## Distribution Plan
This remains a hosted web product delivered through the existing Synchrono City client and Concierge backend. Existing deployment paths cover the web-service portion; the first launch should be a private alpha with one host and one or two study circles rather than a broad release.

- Delivery channel: existing web app plus Concierge-backed APIs
- Initial rollout: one founding organizer, one city, multiple recurring study circles under that organizer
- Operational model: existing hostable stack, likely through the current deployment path or local operator-run setup
- Publish discipline: operator checklist for host cadence, room norms, office-hours schedule, circle separation, and in-person follow-up
- Expansion path: other organizers can later host their own circles under their own relay/operator control without losing portable identity or memory

## Dependencies
- Web work in the World route to make a study-circle beacon legible as a recurring room rather than a generic place card
- World-map interaction work so discovery still feels spatial and map-owned rather than like a directory layered awkwardly on top
- App-state support for richer beacon metadata, host state, office-hours/live-status handling, and host-to-many-circles relationships
- Concierge support if office-hours schedule, room metadata, summaries, or moderation state require persistent APIs
- Real user recruitment, because absent that the product will look like theory wearing UI

## The Assignment
Recruit 5 curiosity-driven adults in one city and run 3 host-led office-hours sessions for the first study circle within 2 weeks. If that works, open a second distinct circle under the same host and watch whether users can tell the difference between the rooms. Measure one thing above all else: after sampling the room online, do they attend the in-person gathering? Do not ask whether they "like the concept." Watch whether curiosity becomes presence.
