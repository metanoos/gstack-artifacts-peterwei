## Problem Statement
Synchrono City should help a serious, intellectually curious local person attend study groups they would otherwise avoid because the room is socially opaque and the commitment cost feels too high.

The first wedge is not gatherings in general. It is recurring reading and study groups, ideally serious enough that a new person cares whether the room is shallow, awkward, performative, or actually worth crossing town for.

The product should not add a fake preview session or a separate "meet people online first" layer. The real move is to make the actual room hybrid, so a first-timer can attend remotely, understand how the room feels, and later decide whether to show up in person.

## Demand Evidence
Current demand evidence is founder-led, not market-proven.

- The founder gave a concrete first-person signal: a 33-year-old in the San Fernando Valley, helping his mom with landlord work, walked past a gathering in a Vons parking lot, felt anxious about interrupting an unknown group, and did not engage.
- The current fallback after these missed opportunities is specific and costly: go home, lurk X and YouTube, and feel the pain of a missed social/intellectual opportunity.
- The founder named the incumbent workflow directly: Meetup listings exist, but users still have to squint at the description and guess the vibe of the room.
- The founder rejected one plausible but wrong product thesis: "meet people virtually before meeting them in the field" did not sound appealing. That sharpened the wedge. The right move is attending the actual room remotely first.
- Generalized web search supported the mechanism. Church operators already build "plan your visit" surfaces to reduce first-visit uncertainty. Mental-health material from NIMH supports that fear of judgment and unfamiliar social situations causes avoidance. This is not proof of product demand, but it does support the underlying behavior.

Evidence gap:

- No external user has yet said they would be upset if this disappeared.
- No host has yet committed to running a recurring hybrid study room with real newcomers.
- No one has yet demonstrated that remote-first attendance materially increases later in-person attendance.

## Status Quo
Today the workaround is thin and mostly solitary:

1. A person sees a local gathering in the wild or finds a study group on Meetup.
2. They try to infer the vibe from the listing text, topic, and maybe a few organizer cues.
3. They still cannot tell whether the room is serious, weird, shallow, socially awkward, or worth the drive.
4. They do not go, or they postpone the decision.
5. They go home and consume passive solo media instead.

The incumbent is not "other study group software." It is:

- Meetup as the bulletin board
- vague event descriptions standing in for real room legibility
- X and YouTube as the passive fallback after the social moment dies

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge
Target attendee:

- A 33-year-old or similar adult in the San Fernando Valley
- Intellectually curious, somewhat socially anxious, serious enough to care about room quality
- Interested in philosophy, religion, literature, or other hard-edged reading groups
- Unwilling to gamble an evening on a room they cannot read in advance

Target host:

- One local organizer who already runs or is willing to run a recurring reading group
- Clear enough in thought to state the room's thesis, norms, and expected tone
- Willing to run the room in hybrid form, not just advertise it

Narrowest wedge:

- One recurring reading group in one city region, SFV first
- One beacon on the World map for that room
- The beacon shows host thesis, norms, cadence, recent notes, and whether the room is live
- A newcomer can attend the real session remotely, muted by default, and listen before speaking
- If the room feels real, they save the beacon and later attend physically

The canonical example from this session is a Schopenhauer reading group. That is not the permanent brand constraint. It is a useful signal of room seriousness.

## Constraints
- Must keep Synchrono City's map-native thesis intact. The World map remains the primary discovery surface.
- Must keep place first-class. Even hybrid rooms should remain attached to a specific recurring place.
- Must not become generic remote-group software or "Zoom with nicer copy."
- Must not require a separate preview session before the actual gathering.
- Must let first-time attendees join with low social pressure: muted by default, no cold-calling, easy exit.
- Must make the room legible through real signals: host voice, notes, live attendance state, norms, attendee mix.
- Must work with sparse early supply. One serious host and one room is enough to start.
- Must reuse existing repo primitives where possible: map beacons, marker cards, threads, call overlay, app-state bootstrap, Concierge policy boundaries.
- Must optimize for learning whether remote-first attendance increases later physical attendance.

## Premises
1. The first viable product is for serious study groups, not gatherings in general.
2. The first user is someone intellectually curious, somewhat socially anxious, and unwilling to walk into an unknown room cold.
3. Discovery alone is not the job. The job is room legibility plus low-commitment participation in the actual room.
4. The online mode should be hybrid attendance for the real room, not a separate "preview" social product.
5. The main success outcome is later in-person attendance, but the remote mode must still be worth using on its own.

## Approaches Considered
### Approach A: Legibility-Only Study Beacon
Create a richer study-group beacon with host thesis, norms, who usually attends, recent notes, and schedule, but no live remote attendance initially.

Pros:
- Fastest to ship
- Directly improves on Meetup descriptions
- Strong reuse of existing map, beacon, and thread surfaces

Cons:
- Still asks copy to stand in for a real room
- May not cross the trust gap for hesitant users
- Does not test the strongest behavior from this session

### Approach B: Hybrid Study Room
Make the actual study group hybrid by default. A new user can attend remotely first, then decide later whether to show up physically.

Pros:
- Best fit with the founder's own behavior and stated preference
- Keeps one room, one host, one discussion, one shared memory
- Builds a stronger bridge from passive lurking to real participation
- More defensible than a generic listing upgrade

Cons:
- Host burden is higher
- Audio/video quality and moderation become part of product quality
- Consent and room norms matter more because the live room is the product

### Approach C: Observe-First Room
Let users inspect the room through clips, summaries, or listen-only access before ever joining fully.

Pros:
- Lower friction than full hybrid attendance
- Easier than two-way live participation
- Still improves room legibility

Cons:
- Can drift into voyeurism or passive consumption
- Gives weaker signal than attending the live room
- Risks solving curiosity while failing to create belonging

## Recommended Approach
Choose Approach B, Hybrid Study Room.

This is the first version that directly matches the founder truth uncovered in the session:

> "i want to attend virtually before meeting these people in person"

That is stronger than "meet people online first" and stronger than "read a better event listing." The product should not invent a separate preview ritual. It should let a new user attend the actual room from home, hear how people think, understand the host's tone, and later decide whether that room is worth showing up to physically.

Recommended product shape:

1. The World tab remains map-first.
2. Each serious reading group appears as a beacon, not a generic event pin.
3. Opening the beacon reveals room thesis, norms, cadence, recent notes, and live status.
4. If the room is live, a newcomer can attend remotely with a low-pressure default posture.
5. The chat rail and notes give additional room signal, but the live room remains the strongest trust surface.
6. If the room earns the trip, the user saves the beacon and later shows up in person.

## Open Questions
- Should remote-first attendees appear with stable identity, pseudonym, or a lighter guest mode?
- Is audio-only enough for first visits, or does the room need optional video to feel real?
- How much of the room should be visible before joining: current participants, recurring attendee mix, past excerpts, host notes?
- Does every study room need an explicit host thesis, or is that only necessary for more serious/intellectual circles?
- Should the host be able to designate some sessions as hybrid and others as in-person only?
- How should the product distinguish "serious room" from "casual meetup" without becoming snobbish or fake-academic?

## Success Criteria
- Recruit one real host and run 3 recurring hybrid study sessions in the SFV area.
- Get 5 to 10 curious locals to inspect the beacon and at least attend remotely once.
- At least 70% of trial users report that the live room gave materially better signal than the event description alone.
- At least 40% of remote-first attendees return for either a second remote session or an in-person visit.
- At least 2 remote-first attendees later attend in person.
- The host wants to keep running the room because the hybrid mode improved attendance quality rather than diluting it.

## Distribution Plan
This remains a hosted web product delivered through the existing Synchrono City client and Concierge backend. The initial rollout should be private alpha, not a public launch.

- Delivery channel: the existing web app, with room state and trust signals integrated into the World route and call surfaces
- Rollout: one host, one room, one local geography first
- Operational model: founder-run pilot using the current hostable stack
- Expansion path: once the loop works for study groups, widen to adjacent room types carefully, without losing the core "real room first" mechanic

## Dependencies
- World-route changes to make study-group beacons legible as recurring rooms
- Marker-card and/or right-rail changes to surface host thesis, norms, live status, and room notes
- App-state changes to represent hybrid room state and newcomer-friendly attendance defaults
- Call-overlay and LiveKit flow adjustments for remote-first attendance behavior
- Concierge support only where persistent room metadata, access rules, or moderation state require server backing
- Real pilot host and real participants, because absent that this is still concept art

## The Assignment
Run the product manually before you build it.

Find or host one serious reading group in the SFV area, Schopenhauer is fine, and run 3 hybrid sessions over 2 to 3 weeks. If no host exists, be the host yourself. Invite 5 to 10 people. Let first-timers attend remotely. Measure one thing above all else: do people who would have stayed home decide later to show up in person?

Do not ask whether they "like the concept." Watch whether remote-first attendance changes real-world participation.
