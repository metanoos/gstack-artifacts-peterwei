## Problem Statement
Synchrono City should help a local aspiring founder in Los Angeles, specifically the San Fernando Valley, discover nearby startup collaborators through low-pressure live conversation that can lead to an in-person meeting. The product thesis is that existing tools fail in different ways: X is passive and non-local, Discord is conversational but not geographically useful, LinkedIn feels performative, and local events only work when people are already in the room. The product's distinctive bet is chosen-place presence: users choose where they want to be discoverable without revealing their actual live location.

## Demand Evidence
Current demand evidence is founder-led, not market-proven.

- The founder described a repeated personal failure mode: bored and alone, opening X to scroll, then opening Discord to see if anyone was around, then ending the session no closer to an in-person relationship or local connection.
- The founder's strongest specific need narrowed from broad social connection into local collaborator discovery: "i want someone to team up with me to build a startup."
- The founder articulated concrete trust requirements: low risk means easy disengagement, low perceived danger, low immediate sexual pressure, and the ability to audit before joining.

Evidence gap:

- No named external user yet.
- No behavior from non-founder users yet.
- No one has demonstrated "I'd be upset if this disappeared tomorrow."

## Status Quo
The current workaround is a broken chain of tools:

1. A lonely or restless local founder opens X and consumes posts passively.
2. They open Discord and may see people online, but those relationships are remote-first and non-geographic.
3. They do not join or deepen conversation because the signal is weak, the social cost is high, and there is no realistic bridge to meeting nearby.
4. They remain online, but make no progress toward building a local startup relationship.

Alternative incumbent tools also fail differently:

- X finds famous or remote people, not nearby peers.
- LinkedIn is high-status and performative, not a low-pressure live conversation surface.
- Local events create occasional density, but they do not help users connect before or between events.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge
Target user:

- A 34-year-old aspiring founder in Los Angeles / SFV who wants to find nearby collaborators to build a startup.
- They are shy enough to hesitate before joining a room, but motivated enough to act if the room feels safe, relevant, and easy to leave.
- They value privacy and do not want to broadcast their actual location.

Narrowest wedge:

- A founder opens the map and sees a handful of nearby founder rooms with explicit context.
- Before joining, they can audit the room's topic, mission, host intent, vibe, and norms.
- They can join muted and listen first.
- If the conversation is good, the product creates a lightweight handoff, "meet again" or "coffee pact," so the session can turn into a local in-person meeting.

This is narrower than "geo-social platform" and broader than "dating app." It is a trust-ladder product for nearby founder discovery.

## Constraints
- Must preserve chosen-place presence rather than actual-location reporting.
- Must feel low-risk for shy users: easy exit, low commitment, clear expectations before joining.
- Must work with sparse early supply in one city region, likely LA/SFV first.
- Must reuse the current repository shape where possible: map-owned World route, global call overlay, bootstrap-fed app state, and Concierge-backed API boundaries.
- Must not assume broad two-sided liquidity on day one.
- Must support collaboration-first positioning without blocking future expansion into friendship, dating, or business meetups.

## Premises
1. The first real wedge is local aspiring founders in LA/SFV who want collaborators, not a general-purpose geo-social platform.
2. Chosen-place presence is a core trust advantage and should remain central to the product story.
3. Random map calls alone are insufficient; rooms need structured context before join.
4. Demand is real for the founder, but not yet validated beyond the founder; the first design must optimize for cheap learning.
5. Most of the change belongs in the web product surface and discovery flow, with backend support added only where trust, metadata, and moderation require it.

## Approaches Considered
### Approach A: Curated Founder Rooms, minimal wedge
Show a map with a small set of hand-seeded founder calls in LA/SFV. Each room has a clear title and topic. Users can preview lightly, join muted, talk, then optionally agree to meet later.

Pros:
- Fastest path to a usable test
- Strong reuse of existing World and call surfaces
- Easy to seed manually with 5 to 20 users

Cons:
- Thin trust layer
- Less learning about why rooms convert
- Supply may feel sparse or random

### Approach B: Trust-Ladder Founder Discovery
Every live room exposes mission, topic, host intent, audience, and norms before join. Users can preview, join muted, listen first, then participate. A successful room can end with a lightweight follow-up mechanism that turns a good call into a local next step.

Pros:
- Best fit for the founder's stated risk model
- Sharpest wedge for collaboration-first local discovery
- Creates clearer product learning around trust and conversion

Cons:
- Requires more UI and metadata than the minimal wedge
- Needs stronger moderation and room-creation rules
- Slightly slower than the simplest version

### Approach C: Async-to-Live Founder Radar
Users publish collaboration intents on the map first, like "looking for GTM cofounder" or "want to jam on civic tooling." Interested locals opt in, then the system creates a live room or scheduled call.

Pros:
- Lower social risk than dropping straight into live calls
- Better fit for thin-market density
- Makes collaboration intent explicit

Cons:
- Less immediate and magical
- More workflow before the key moment
- Risks feeling like a networking board instead of a live product

## Recommended Approach
Choose Approach B, Trust-Ladder Founder Discovery.

This is the first version most aligned with the actual user truth uncovered in the session. The founder does not merely want a map full of live calls. They want a low-risk path into local collaboration. That means pre-join trust cues are not polish, they are the product. The minimal wedge is good for speed, but it leaves too much ambiguity exactly where shy users need help. The async-first approach is safer, but it moves too far away from the "see nearby activity and join now" magic that makes Synchrono City distinct.

Recommended product shape:

1. World map shows a small number of live founder beacons in LA/SFV.
2. Each beacon is represented by an avatar marker. When opened, the beacon card should emerge with its top-left corner anchored at that avatar, reinforcing the beacon as the primary spatial object.
3. The primary closed-state beacon actions are `Open` and `Join call`. Once a beacon is already open, the visible action should become `Close`.
4. `Open` should populate the right rail with the selected beacon's text-thread style chat, closer to a Discord text channel than a generic info panel. When no beacon is selected, that rail should list nearby beacons instead.
5. The beacon card itself should stay minimal: beacon name, beacon about, and the live call block. Do not duplicate descriptions, chips, or onboarding copy there.
6. The live call block should show `LIVE - <timer>` above the participant roster.
7. The participant roster should include picture/avatar, display name, abbreviated `npub`, and live media-state indicators for mic, camera, and screenshare.
8. `Join call` should join muted by default, without repeating that fact in button copy on every beacon.
9. In-call interaction includes low-pressure participation controls, like raise hand or unmute when ready.
10. Helper copy should be onboarding-level guidance rather than repeated per-beacon explanation. The bottom helper surface should explain the system once, not duplicate the beacon card.
11. Header chips that pretend the user is in a fixed local mode are unnecessary on a global map and should be removed unless they communicate something operationally important.
12. Once a beacon is opened, the right rail should fully switch into opened-beacon mode rather than continuing to show nearby beacons underneath.
13. The opened-beacon panel should behave like a real text channel, closer to Discord chat than stacked info cards. It should support reactions on messages.
14. When one user sends multiple messages within a short time window, avatar, username, and visible timestamp should appear only on the first message in the group. Later messages in that run should reveal their timestamp on hover.
15. The opened-beacon panel should include a text input and send button pinned to the bottom so the rail clearly reads as an active text-thread surface.
16. The bottom onboarding strip should use concise language that explains the trust model without sounding like product marketing copy.
17. After a good session, users can express lightweight mutual follow-up intent, for example `Meet again` or `Coffee pact`, but that prompt should appear at session end rather than in the default discovery layout.
18. The opened-beacon rail header should use the beacon title itself rather than a generic label like `Opened beacon`.
19. The opened-beacon rail should expose a `Settings` action for viewing and editing beacon policy and metadata, and a `People` action for browsing members and admins.
20. Live beacon avatars on the map should pulse so live activity is legible without changing the marker's identity.
21. The beacon panel should render the beacon picture/avatar at the top-left of the panel so identity is carried consistently from the map marker into the opened state.
22. Beacons should also appear in the `Chats` tab as part of a universal inbox that includes beacons, DMs, and group DMs.
23. On desktop, `Chats` should use a Mac iMessage-like split view: conversation list on the left, active conversation on the right.
24. In that universal inbox, selecting a beacon thread should open the same beacon conversation surface used elsewhere, rather than a second parallel UI.
25. On mobile, opening any beacon, DM, or group DM conversation should take over the screen and replace split view with a back/close affordance.
26. The app should have a persistent top app bar with `Synchrono.City` branding at top-left and tab controls at top-right for `World`, `Chats`, `Pulse`, and `Settings`.
27. The World tab must remain map-first: map on the left, opened beacon panel on the right. The Chats split-view belongs to the Chats tab, not the World tab.
28. When a call is active, a global live-call overlay should appear as a bottom bar across the app.
29. That bottom call bar should show the active media streams/participants inside it, along with icon controls for mic, camera, screenshare, and a clear `Leave call` action.

## Open Questions
- Should early rooms be operator-curated, host-created, or both?
- Is video on by default too socially expensive for the first wedge?
- What minimal moderation and reporting surfaces are required before real user testing?
- Should the first launch focus on live-now rooms only, or include scheduled rooms as fallback for low density?
- What exact trust signal best predicts conversion: host identity, topic precision, room norms, mutual connections, or previous attendance?

## Success Criteria
- At least 5 to 10 local founder-type users in LA/SFV agree to try the product.
- At least 3 live rooms happen with clear topic framing.
- At least 60% of invited users who open the product join at least one room.
- At least 2 sessions end with explicit mutual follow-up intent.
- At least 1 pair agrees to meet in person after first contact.
- Qualitative outcome: users report that the pre-join context made the room feel safer and more worth trying.

## Distribution Plan
This remains a hosted web product delivered through the existing Synchrono City client and Concierge backend. Early testing should run as a city-scoped private alpha rather than a public launch.

- Delivery channel: existing web app plus concierge-backed APIs
- Initial rollout: hand-invited LA/SFV founder cohort
- Operational model: use the current hostable stack, likely via Docker Compose or the existing deployment path once the product flow is ready
- Publish discipline: add a lightweight operator checklist for room seeding, moderation, and follow-up tracking before each alpha session

## Dependencies
- Web product work in the World route, room preview UI, and pre-join flow
- App-state support for richer room metadata and follow-up state
- Concierge support if room metadata, host intent, moderation state, or post-call follow-up need persistent APIs
- Early user recruitment in LA/SFV, otherwise the product will look dead regardless of design quality

## The Assignment
Recruit 5 local aspiring founders in LA/SFV and run 3 manually curated sessions within 2 weeks. Do not ask whether the idea sounds good. Ask each person to actually join a founder room, then measure whether they stayed, spoke, and wanted a follow-up. The next truth is in behavior, not opinion.

## What I noticed about how you think
- You kept returning to privacy from different angles, especially "you choose where to be, rather than reporting your actual location," which is probably the deepest product instinct in the whole session.
- Your strongest concrete motivation was not abstract loneliness but "i want someone to team up with me to build a startup."
- When the wedge got muddy, you still defended the broader meetup vision, but you were willing to narrow the first-session outcome to local collaboration.
- You surfaced the real trust requirement in plain language: audit before joining, easy disengagement, and a room where people are actually there to talk.
