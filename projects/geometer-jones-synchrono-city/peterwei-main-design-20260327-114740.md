## Problem Statement
Synchrono City should help a beginner who wants to understand neural nets make real progress in a local room they can inspect before committing to show up in person.

The first wedge is not "AI community" and not a generic study group platform. It is one recurring beginner neural nets cohort, anchored to a real place, where a cautious newcomer can understand the room, join with low social pressure, and make visible week-by-week progress.

The educational promise matters, but only in the context of a real local room. This should not become courseware on a map. The real thesis is that beginner learning gets easier when the room is legible, the sequence is finite, and first attendance is low-risk.

The concrete backbone for the first version is Andrej Karpathy's `Neural Networks: Zero to Hero` sequence, used as the canonical curriculum for a 4-week beginner cohort.

## Demand Evidence
Current demand evidence is founder-led, not market-proven.

- The founder named himself as the first user instead of hiding behind broad categories.
- The founder's actual desire is specific: he wants a book-club-like room to study neural nets, philosophy, and related subjects, but the session forced the first wedge down to one room with one learning promise.
- The strongest revealed pattern is not abstract interest in AI. It is the need to understand whether a room will be welcoming, serious, and worth the trip before showing up.
- The current fallback is concrete: instead of joining a real room, people lurk Discord, X, YouTube, and Twitch, or they try generic meetup surfaces that do not tell them what the room really feels like.
- Generalized search supported the premise that incumbent tools over-index on discovery and logistics while under-serving first-visit uncertainty. Meetup guidance emphasizes discoverability and event metadata. Church onboarding guidance emphasizes reducing uncertainty for first-time visitors. Hybrid book-club material frames hybrid as access and flexibility, but not as a trust mechanism for crossing the in-person gap.

Evidence gap:

- No external attendee has yet said they would be upset if this disappeared.
- No host besides the founder has yet committed to running the cohort.
- No one has yet shown that a hybrid beginner cohort increases later in-person attendance or completion.

## Status Quo
The current workaround is fragmented and weak:

1. A curious beginner wants to understand neural nets, but does not want to walk cold into an unknown room.
2. They consume solo material online: Karpathy videos, YouTube explainers, X threads, Discord chatter, or scattered tutorials.
3. If they search for in-person options, they get broad meetup listings, generic AI groups, or event descriptions that still do not answer whether the room is too advanced, awkward, shallow, or worth crossing town for.
4. They postpone, lurk, or stay home.
5. Learning remains solitary and inconsistent, and no local cohort forms around repeated embodied participation.

The incumbent is not "another neural nets cohort platform." It is:

- solo online learning
- generic event discovery
- vague room descriptions standing in for real legibility
- ambient parasocial consumption instead of accountable beginner progress

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge
Target attendee:

- A beginner in one local geography, SFV is the natural first example
- Curious enough to study neural nets seriously, but not confident enough to gamble an evening on a room they cannot read in advance
- Wants structured progress, not just vibes, networking, or AI hype
- May prefer to attend remotely first before deciding whether to come in person

Target host:

- The founder initially, or one trusted helper capable of leading a beginner-friendly sequence
- Can explain neural nets without turning the room into status theater
- Willing to host a recurring hybrid room tied to one real place

Narrowest wedge:

- One beginner neural nets cohort in one local geography
- One beacon on the World map for that cohort
- One 4-week sequence built on `Neural Networks: Zero to Hero`
- One concept and one tiny exercise per week
- One low-pressure hybrid session each week where a first-timer can join muted, listen, and decide whether to return physically
- One shared artifact per week so progress is visible and cumulative

This is much narrower than "AI learning community" and much stronger than "book club for smart topics."

## Constraints
- Must preserve Synchrono City's map-native thesis. Discovery still starts from the World map, not from a flat course catalog.
- Must keep place first-class. Even a hybrid cohort stays attached to a real recurring location.
- Must not become generic LMS software, courseware, or Zoom-with-copy.
- Must let first-timers attend with low social pressure: muted by default, easy exit, no cold-call expectation.
- Must make beginner level explicit so the room does not feel like expert theater.
- Must make progress legible: syllabus, current week, tiny exercises, shared artifacts, and what comes next.
- Must keep the online layer subordinate to the real room, not let it drift into a pure content surface.
- Must reuse the current stack where possible: World beacons, marker cards, thread memory, uploads, call overlay, Concierge policy boundary.
- Must optimize for learning whether hybrid first attendance increases completion and later in-person attendance.

## Premises
1. The first product is one serious recurring beginner neural nets cohort, not a general-purpose local social network.
2. The first user is the founder and people like him: intellectually curious, somewhat cautious, and unwilling to gamble an evening on an opaque room.
3. The core job is room legibility plus low-pressure first attendance, not just event discovery.
4. The online layer should attach to the real room, not become a separate parasocial content surface.
5. The first room must pick one thesis and one finite sequence, not five topics at once.
6. Success means more real repeat attendance and visible beginner progress in one local geography, not more ambient lurking.

## Approaches Considered
### Approach A: Hybrid Reading Circle
A recurring beginner neural nets room centered on reading and discussion only. People can attend in person or remotely, but there is no explicit multi-week curriculum or progress trail.

Pros:
- Fastest to launch
- Low host prep
- Reuses existing map and room mechanics heavily

Cons:
- The learning promise stays vague
- Harder for a newcomer to know what "done" looks like
- Risks drifting back into a smart-people room with no visible beginner progress

### Approach B: Hybrid Beginner Cohort
A 4-week beginner neural nets cohort anchored to one place. Each week has one concept, one tiny exercise, one live hybrid session, and one shared artifact. The product promise is: you will make real beginner progress with other people nearby.

Pros:
- Best fit with the founder's actual need and chosen posture
- Makes the beginner promise concrete and finite
- Gives newcomers a clear reason to join now rather than someday
- Preserves the room while creating accountability and cumulative learning
- Strong reuse of existing beacon, live session, and memory surfaces

Cons:
- Host burden is higher than a loose discussion room
- Requires explicit session design and progress tracking
- AV quality, moderation, and beginner friendliness become first-order product concerns

### Approach C: Applied Build Lab
A hybrid cohort where the goal is shipping tiny models or demos fast. More hands-on and exciting, but it assumes more host capability and risks losing true beginners.

Pros:
- High energy and tangible output
- Could create stronger retention for technically ambitious users
- Produces visible artifacts quickly

Cons:
- Too advanced for the stated first user
- Higher intimidation and host burden
- Can mutate into a project club before trust and beginner onboarding are solved

## Recommended Approach
Choose Approach B, Hybrid Beginner Cohort.

This is the cleanest first wedge because it preserves the real insight from the session: the product is not generic education and not generic social discovery. It is a legible local room that helps a true beginner make actual progress.

The Karpathy curriculum makes the first cohort more credible because the sequence already exists and is respected. Synchrono City should not invent educational authority from scratch. It should host a better social and place-based wrapper around a known-good beginner path.

Recommended product shape:

1. The World tab remains map-first.
2. One beacon represents the cohort at its recurring place.
3. Opening the beacon answers four questions immediately: what is this, who is it for, what week are we on, and how risky is it to join tonight?
4. The room surface shows host posture, beginner level, current concept, next session, and prior cohort artifacts.
5. When live, a first-timer can join remotely in a low-pressure mode and observe the actual room.
6. Between sessions, the beacon keeps shared notes, tiny exercise prompts, and cohort memory.
7. The primary success path is not "consume content." It is "join, progress, return, then show up in person."

## Open Questions
- Should first-time remote attendees appear under stable identity, pseudonym, or a lighter guest mode?
- Is 4 weeks the right beginner window, or should the first cohort be 3 or 6 weeks?
- Should the shared artifact each week live as a note thread, a Blossom upload, a linked repo, or all three?
- How much math should be expected up front before the room stops feeling beginner-safe?
- Should the host explicitly ban status signaling like interrupt-heavy flexing and jargon dumping?
- Does the room need a waitlist or cap to preserve beginner quality once the signal gets stronger?

## Success Criteria
- Recruit 5 to 10 beginner-curious people in one local geography for the first cohort.
- Run all 4 sessions with the host reliably present.
- At least 70% of participants report that the room felt legible before attending.
- At least 60% of participants complete 3 of the 4 weeks.
- At least 2 remote-first participants later attend in person.
- At least 3 participants finish the cohort saying they now concretely understand concepts they previously only recognized as words.
- The host wants to run cohort 2 because the format improved both attendance quality and learning clarity.

## Distribution Plan
This remains a hosted web product delivered through the existing Synchrono City client and Concierge backend. The first launch should be a private alpha, not a public release.

- Delivery channel: the existing web app, with cohort-specific room state integrated into the World route and live session surfaces
- Rollout: one founder-run cohort, one local place, one start date, one curriculum sequence
- Operational model: founder-run pilot using the current hostable stack
- Expansion path: if the beginner neural nets cohort works, add adjacent cohorts carefully without losing the local-room and legibility thesis

## Dependencies
- World-route changes so a cohort beacon reads like a finite beginner room, not a generic place pin
- Marker-card or detail-surface changes to show level, week number, host posture, and next-session clarity
- App-state additions for cohort metadata, progress state, and beginner-safe live attendance defaults
- Call-overlay and LiveKit flow changes to better support remote-first observation and easy return
- Memory surfaces for weekly artifacts, prompts, and summaries
- Real cohort recruitment, because without that this remains a clean theory wrapped in UI

## The Assignment
Do the manual pilot before you overbuild the software.

Pick one real place in the SFV. Schedule one 4-week beginner cohort using Karpathy's `Neural Networks: Zero to Hero`. Recruit 5 to 10 people who are curious but truly early. Run the first cohort manually if you have to, even if parts of the software are still rough.

Measure three things only:
1. Did people join the first session?
2. Did they come back for week 2?
3. Did any remote-first person later show up in person?

Do not ask whether they "like the concept." Watch whether structured local learning changes actual behavior.
